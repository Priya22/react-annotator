1. Introduction

Welcome! The purpose of this tool is to annotate quotations within a text. We are collecting the following data for each quote:
  • Type of quote (implicit, anaphoric or explicit).
  • Speaker of the quote.
  • Addressee (the person or persons to whom the quotation is addressed)
  • The referring expression; the phrase, given by the narrator outside of quotation, that indicates the speaker and, sometimes, the addressee. (Note that implicit quotes don't have a referring expression.)
  • Mentions within the quotation; characters mentioned in the quotation, whether by name or through a pronoun (e.g., "She told me Rob was ill")

1.1 Types of quotation

The type of quotation can be explicit, anaphoric, or implicit.

An explicit quotation will include a referring expression that provides the speaker’s name as a proper noun. For example, 

                "But you forget, mamma," said Elizabeth, "that we shall meet him at the assemblies, and that Mrs. Long promised to introduce him."

An anaphoric quotation employs a pronoun in the referring expression. 

    "We are not in a way to know what Mr. Bingley likes," said her mother resentfully, "since we are not to visit."

An implicit quotation uses no referring expression at all. The bolded quotation in the following is an example of an implicit quotation (Mr. Bennet is speaking, but no referring expression is given): 

        “Do you not want to know who has taken it?” cried his wife impatiently.
        “You want to tell me, and I have no objection to hearing it.”
        This was invitation enough.

1.2 Additional notes on mentions

Only annotate mentions within quotations.

Only annotate mentions of specific characters. Do not annotate mentions of abstractions (in “It is the role of a good man to do his duty,” leave “his” un-annotated) or of anything that is not a character.

Do not annotate first-person pronouns within quotations. Do annotate second- and third-person pronouns within quotations. For example, in “‘I am so cross with you,’ said Frederic,” only annotate the mention of “you”; not “I.”

When a mention is given as a pronoun modifying a noun, annotate the pronoun/noun phrase as one unit. In “I told your sister to leave me alone,” annotate “your sister” as a single entity.

In the case of group entities such as “her sisters,”

  • When each of the characters to whom this expression refers is what you could consider a major character, provide annotate all of these characters. One test for whether someone is a “major character” is whether they speak in the novel, though there may be cases in which a “major character” never speaks. (Think of Godot!)
  • In a case like “Mrs. Long’s nieces” near the start of Pride and Prejudice, where the nieces are clearly not major characters, only annotate the mention of Mrs. Long, and leave “nieces” un-annotated

1.3 Additional notes on quotations

Quotations should be evaluated in their full context, and not just on a paragraph-by-paragraph basis. Consider the following example: 

    Observing his second daughter employed in trimming a hat, he suddenly addressed her with:
    “I hope Mr. Bingley will like it, Lizzy.”
Even though no referring expression is given in the paragraph in which it appears, the bolded passage should be recorded as “anaphoric,” because it is introduced by a referring expression in the previous paragraph.

Similarly, in multi-paragraph quotations, referring expressions will not appear with each paragraph. Annotate these multi-paragraph quotations by considering them as a single unit. Some paragraphs will contain more than one type of quotation. Each of these should be annotated separately. Consider the following: 

    “I am totally fed up with you, Margaret,” she said. “And I’ve had it with you, too, Richard.”
Although they share the same referring expression, the two bolded phrases should be annotated separately, since they have different addressees.

By contrast, in the following example, both bolded passages can be annotated together: 

    “I am totally fed up with the both of you,” she said angrily. “You’re both driving me absolutely crazy.”

Do not include adverbs when marking referring expressions. In the above quotation, do not include “angrily.” However, when an adverb is wedged into the centre of a referring expression, include it. In the following example, include the full bolded passage as the referring expression 

    “I’m fed up with the both of you,” she said angrily to Margaret and Richard.

Do not annotate apparent referring expressions that come in the quotation itself (for instance, “Listen, Richard, I’ve had it with you”). Referring expressions as we understand them can only be given by the narrator and so can only come outside of quotations.

1.4 Character names and aliases

Some characters will be referred to by many names. For instance, Kitty Bennet in Pride and Prejudice is called “Kitty,” “Catherine,” “Miss Bennet,” and so on.
For our purposes, it does not matter which of these names you consider the principal name and which an alias. The important thing is that there is only one entry in the character list for each character and that you record all of their aliases.

1.5 Sample passage and annotation

Consider the following passage: 

    "My dear Mr. Bennet," said his lady to him one day, "have you heard that Netherfield Park is let at last?"
    Mr. Bennet replied that he had not.
    "But it is," returned she "for Mrs. Long has just been here, and she told me all about it."
Suppose we are annotating the bolded quotation. The data we wish to obtain is the following:
  • Type of Quote: Anaphoric
  • Speaker: Mrs. Bennet
  • Addressee: Mr. Bennet
  • Referring expression: returned she
  • Mentions:
    • Mrs. Long: Mrs. Long
    • she: Mrs. Long

2. Annotation Tool Components

  • From the landing page, click the "Choose files" button. Navigate to the 'data/' folder of the root directory and choose the text file that you wish to annotate.
  • The selected text, along with a list of initial characters will load on the main page.
  • The main components of the tool are as follows:
    • Tabs: There are two tabs, Quotes and Mentions, on the top right of the text area. You can click on each of them to switch between annotating quote information (i.e, type, speaker, addressee and referring expression) and mention information (referred entity).
    • Highlighted text: An initial set of quotes and mentions have been automatically extracted and highlighted in the text area. Note that these may not be accurate or complete. Please ensure you are annotating the correct portion of the text.
    • Character List: The leftmost partition contains a list of characters and their aliases that have also been automatically extracted. Note that these may not be accurate or complete. You can add/delete both character names as well as their aliases.
    • Information collection: When you click on a highlighted quote or mention, the middle partition will display information and instructions to facilitate the annotation.
    • Save Progress: Please ensure you are continually saving your annotations to file. Annotations, once completed, are automatically saved; and this is just a precautionary measure.
    • Reload: Click on this button after saving to reload a new file to annotate.

3. Highlights: Color codes

As you progress through the annotation task, the color of the quotations/mentions change to indicate the phase of annotation you are in. The following list describes them for quotes, but is equally applicable to mentions.

  • Yellow: This is an identified quote. It has not yet been annotated.
  • Grey: This is a quote that is currently selected to be annotated.
  • Blue: This quote has been annotated.

4. Annotation Process

(Replace "quote" with "mention" wherever applicable.)

A quote needs to be identified before it can be annotated. To identify a portion of the text as a quote, simply select that portion with your cursor.

To start annotating an identified quote, proceed as follows:

  1. Click on the span of text that has been highlighted. It should appear in the middle partition of the tool, under "Current selection".
  2. Multiple quotes can be annotated at the same time. To add more quotes to the current selection, simply click on them. To remove a selected quote, click on it again.
  3. Quote type: Select the correct option (Implicit, Anaphoric, or Explicit) from the radio-buttons.
  4. Speaker selection: Each quote is assumed to have one speaker. Click the Edit button to select the speaker. The character list on the right will change it's appearance to display selectable radio-buttons next to each character. Check the radio button for the correct speaker, and click on the Submit button for Speaker. You will see the selected character has been identified as the speaker.
  5. Addressee selection: Each quote may have multiple addressees. Click the Edit button to select them. The character list on the right will change it's appearance to display selectable checkboxes next to each character. Check the checkbox for the correct addressee(s), and click on the Submit button. You will see the selected character(s) displayed as addressee(s).
  6. Referring expression: This field will only appear for Anaphoric and Explicit quotes. To select the expression, first click on the Edit button. Then select the correct portion of the text from the text-area on the right, and click Submit. The selected text will be displayed as the referring expression.
  7. For mentions: The annotation for mentions consists only of selecting the referred entities from the character list on the left. This is similar to the Addressee annotation.
  8. Be sure to click “Submit Info” when you are done annotating a particular quotation or group of quotations. If you don’t, it/they will remain selected, and havoc will ensue.

Additional Notes

  1. If any part of the text was incorrectly identified by us as being a mention or a quote, please remove it by selecting the text and clicking the Clear button instead of OK
  2. The character list is automatically extracted and has a high probability of being incomplete as well as redundant, and sometimes wrong. Add, delete, and edit characters as well as their aliases to ensure uniformity.
